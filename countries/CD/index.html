The Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC), formerly Zaire, was home to powerful kingdoms such as the Kongo and Luba before Belgian colonization in 1885. Gaining independence in 1960, it soon fell into political turmoil and dictatorship under Mobutu Sese Seko. The late 20th century saw devastating civil wars, involving multiple African nations. Despite vast mineral wealth, the DRC struggles with conflict and governance issues but remains crucial to Africa’s economic landscape.

The Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) is a vast country located in Central Africa, bordered by nine countries including Angola, Rwanda, and Uganda. Covering approximately 2.34 million square kilometers, it is the second-largest country in Africa by land area. As of 2023, the DRC has an estimated population of over 95 million people. The capital and largest city is Kinshasa. The official language is French, with regional languages such as Lingala, Swahili, Kikongo, and Tshiluba also widely spoken. The DRC operates as a semi-presidential republic. Rich in natural resources, particularly minerals like cobalt and copper, the country faces challenges such as political instability and infrastructure deficits. The DRC is a member of the United Nations, the African Union, and the Southern African Development Community.
