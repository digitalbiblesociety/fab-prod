Chalmers-Schaub Wenli New Testament Рђћ Classical Chinese NT 1897 (China)

Overview

Т╣Џу┤ёу┐░№╝ЇжЪХуЉфС║ГТќЄуљєсђіТќ░у┤ётЁеТЏИсђІ (Chalmers-Schaub Wenli New Testament) is the complete New Testament translated by John Chalmers (1822РђЊ1899) and Martin Schaub (1850РђЊ1900), originally published in 1897 in Wenli (ТќЄуљє) Рђћ the literary form of Classical Chinese Рђћ and digitized by Simon Wong for the United Bible Societies in 2018 (digital edition ┬Е UBS 2018, Public Domain). Available via YouVersion/Bible.com (ID 2212). This is a historical scholarly Bible edition in the classical register of written Chinese (Wenli, ТќЄуљє), which was used in 19th-century missionary Bible translations for educated Chinese readers regardless of regional dialect.

Language and People

The Chalmers-Schaub NT is in Wenli (ТќЄуљє), a formal written register of Chinese based on Classical Chinese (w├Еny├Аn, ТќЄУеђ), used in literary and official communication throughout East Asia. Wenli served as a supra-dialectal written standard understood by literate Chinese speakers of all regions Рђћ Mandarin, Cantonese, Hokkien, Shanghainese, and beyond Рђћ as well as by educated readers in Japan, Korea, and Vietnam. The ISO code cmn (Mandarin) is used here though Wenli technically transcends any single spoken dialect.

Historical Context

John Chalmers (1822РђЊ1899) was a London Missionary Society (LMS) scholar-missionary based in Hong Kong, renowned for his Chinese language scholarship. Martin Schaub (1850РђЊ1900) was a Basel Mission missionary in South China. The 1897 Wenli NT was part of the broader 19th-century Chinese Bible translation project that culminated in the Union Version (1919), which eventually superseded the Wenli style translations. The Wenli NT tradition includes several competing 19th-century translations (Bridgman-Culbertson, Delegates' Version, etc.); the Chalmers-Schaub represents one scholarly lineage. The digital edition was prepared by Simon Wong and made available through the United Bible Societies, preserving this historical text for contemporary digital access.
